title: Less is more
---

The other day Wife and I made a pact to each other - we're going to buy fewer, but better, things. The more I thought bout it, I think this strategy can also apply to life.

To start, Wife and I came to this agreement after attempting to declutter our house and noticing how many things we have but no longer use. Too many spur-of-the-moment purchases. Too many wall hangings bought at Bed, Bath, & Beyond that we liked at the time but a year later were put away in the garage. In other words, we knew we needed to become smarter shoppers. One example: buying two Amazon Fire tablets (one for each of our kids) was a terrible decision in hindsight. If I could do it again I'd buy one iPad for both of them to share. The Amazon tablets are useless at this point. Two years after getting them they're too slow, have no available memory left after all the required updates (and bloatware), and transferring games from the cloud to each kid's profile requires a PhD. On the other hand, iPads just work.

So the more I thought about it, the more this situation reminded me of my own life. I've realized the past few months that having more of a good thing isn't always better. The past few years I've been working a 9-5 job that gets me home everyday and allows me to spend lots of time with the Wife and kids. But lately I think it's actually been a bad thing for me. Let's pause here, this is a hard thing for someone to say....that having too much time with their family has been bad for them. But let me expound on this - the more time I have to spend at home, the more I've found myself falling into an undisciplined rut and becoming lazy. When this happens my creeping depression isn't far behind and soon enough I'm a miserable person to be and be around.

The old saying "you can't help others if you don't help yourself" rings true in this situation. At almost 40 yrs old I'm still learning about my mental makeup and how to handle my mind (games). I've learned that structured work is a great thing for me. It keeps me focused, productive, and keeps my creative juices flowing. Idle time, no matter how well my intentions are, never gets used to its fullest potential. This being said, coming home from a 10 hour day of work and spending 2 quality hours with my wife and kids is much better than a 7 hour work day with 5 hours at home.

I'm not saying this is for everyone, but for me - less is much much more.
